what is IncoTerms here?
Incoterms (International Commercial Terms) are a standardized set of trade rules published by the International Chamber of Commerce that define who's responsible for what during a shipment between a buyer and seller — cost, risk, and logistics at each stage of the journey.
In your PurchaseOrders.csv, the IncoTerms column tells you at what point ownership/risk transfers from the supplier to your company, and who pays for what along the way (freight, insurance, customs, etc.).
Common ones you'll likely see in your data (given PortOfLoading is also a column, this is clearly an import-heavy dataset):
IncotermMeaningFOB (Free On Board)Seller delivers goods onto the ship at the port of loading; buyer takes on risk/cost from that point (ocean freight, insurance, etc.)CIF (Cost, Insurance, Freight)Seller pays for shipping and insurance to the destination port; 
risk transfers once loaded, but seller covers more of the costEXW (Ex Works)Buyer takes on almost everything — pickup from the seller's own facilityDDP (Delivered Duty Paid)Seller handles everything, including customs and duties, all the way to buyer's door — max seller 
responsibilityFCA (Free Carrier)Seller delivers to a carrier named by the buyer, risk transfers there


Products csv
UOM - Unit of Measurement
BasePrice_USD - Listed selling price per unit in USD - what you would charge a customer before any discount.
StandardCost_USD - The company's internal cost per unit to acquire/produce the product — used as the cost basis for margin calculations. 
HSCode - An internationally standardized numeric code (usually 6–10 digits) used to classify traded products for customs, tariffs, and trade statistics. 
Every product crossing an international border gets one — customs authorities use it to determine duty rates and regulations. 
It's a real-world field that pairs naturally with IncoTerms and PortOfLoading from your Purchase Orders — all three point to this being an import/export-heavy business. 
ActiveFlag - A boolean indicating whether the product is currently active/sellable (True) or discontinued/retired (False).
IntroducedDate - The date the product was first introduced/launched into the catalog.

Inventory csv
StockQty - Total physical quantity on hand at that warehouse/lot.
AllocatedQty - How much of that stock is already reserved/committed against open sales orders but not yet shipped.
AvailableQty - What's actually free to sell = StockQty − AllocatedQty.
ReorderLevel - The minimum stock threshold — when StockQty drops to or below this, it's time to reorder.
ReorderQty - How much to order when you hit the reorder point (row 0: 1220) — this is the standard reorder batch size, likely tied to supplier MOQs or economic order quantity.
UnitCost_USD - Cost per unit of the stock actually held in this lot (e.g. $465.43/unit). Note this can differ from Products.StandardCost_USD and from what any given PO paid — i
nventory carries whatever cost was booked when that specific lot was received, so different lots of the same product can have different unit costs (classic lot-costing behavior).
TotalValue_USD - StockQty × UnitCost_USD — the dollar value of that inventory line (e.g. 7,442 × $465.43 ≈ $3,463,730, which matches row 0). This is your basis for inventory valuation reporting.
LastRecievedDate - Date this lot was last replenished/received into the warehouse. 
LastIssuedDate - Date stock was last shipped/issued out of this location — tells you how "live" or stale this particular lot is.
ExpiryDate - Shelf-life expiration for the lot, if applicable. It's almost entirely NaN in your sample and typed as Float in the schema — that's a strong signal this product line (pulp, paper) 
mostly doesn't expire, so this column is only populated for the subset of hazardous/perishable SKUs (ties back to IsHazardous in Products).
BelowReorder - Boolean flag: is StockQty at or below ReorderLevel? Like AvailableQty
LastUpdated - Timestamp this inventory record was last refreshed in the source system

7. Inventory Turnover
How fast stock moves. If turnover is high, you're selling through inventory quickly (good — not much cash sitting idle on shelves). If it's low, stock is piling up and tying up money.
In your data: This one needs an assumption, since we don't have a clean "cost of what was actually sold" — so it's an estimate, not an exact number.
8. Inventory Value at Risk
The dollar value of stock that's running low (below the reorder point) right now. Tells you what's about to run out and how much money is exposed to that risk.
In your data: Already fully built — no extra work needed here.
9. Returns Rate
What percentage of orders come back as returns instead of staying as normal sales. High returns can point to quality issues or ordering mistakes.
In your data: Already flagged in your data — just needs to be counted/visualized.
10. Order Status Funnel
A simple breakdown of how many orders are sitting in each stage — confirmed, shipped, delivered, on hold, cancelled. Useful for spotting where orders get stuck.
